# RLHF

A language model trained only to predict the next word is good at sounding
fluent, but not at being helpful, honest, or safe. It has no notion of which of
two reasonable answers a person would actually prefer. Reinforcement learning
from human feedback, or RLHF, is the main way that gap is closed: it teaches the
model to favor the kinds of answers people choose.

## The loop

The core idea is to let human preferences, not just raw text, shape the model.
It works in three stages. First, the model produces several candidate answers to
the same prompt. Second, people compare those answers and say which they prefer.
Third, those preferences are turned into a signal the model can train against, so
it produces more of what people picked and less of what they passed over.

Do this across many prompts and many comparisons, and the model's behavior shifts
toward the qualities the raters kept rewarding: clearer help, more honesty about
what it does not know, fewer evasive or padded answers.

## What the figure is really doing

The figure above is a simplified stand-in, not a real language model. There is no
text generation happening. Each candidate response is drawn from a small fixed
set of templates, and every template carries hidden trait scores, such as how
helpful, honest, concise, or enthusiastic it is. When you pick one, a tiny
preference model nudges its weights toward the traits of the response you chose
and away from the ones you passed over, using a fixed, seeded rule. As those
weights grow, later rounds are steered to offer more responses with the traits
you favor. That is why your choices visibly reshape both the meters and the menu.

## How the real thing works

A real system is heavier in two ways. The comparisons are not turned into rules
by hand; they train a separate reward model, a network that learns to predict
which answer a human would prefer for any prompt. Then the language model itself
is fine-tuned to maximize that predicted reward, usually with a reinforcement
learning algorithm such as PPO, while a penalty keeps it from drifting too far
from the original model and producing gibberish that games the reward.

The catch is that the model learns exactly what the reward captures. If the
feedback rewards confident-sounding answers, it becomes more confident, whether
or not it is more correct. Good RLHF depends on careful, representative human
feedback, which is why so much effort goes into how those comparisons are
collected.

## What the demo shows

Pick the response you prefer in each round. The meters show the current learned
preference weight for each trait: bars grow to the right for traits you favor and
to the left for ones you avoid. Keep favoring the same trait and watch its meter
climb while the candidate responses start trending toward it. Skip prompt moves
on without giving feedback, and Reset clears the learned preferences.
