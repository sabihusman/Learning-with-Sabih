# Decision boundary

Say you have points scattered across a page and each one belongs to
one of two classes, circle or square. Your job is to draw a line such
that circles fall on one side and squares fall on the other. If the
two clusters are cleanly separated, the line is easy; if they overlap,
no line is perfect and the best you can do is a compromise. The line
you settle on is the model's decision boundary, and this topic is
about where it comes from.

Watch the figure Refit. The boundary starts as a straight vertical
line, essentially a guess: the model has no reason yet to prefer any
orientation. Then the loss readout starts falling and the line
rotates. What is running underneath is real logistic regression fit
by gradient descent, the same idea introduced in the first topic of
this section. At each tick the model looks at every point, measures
how badly it is currently classifying them, and nudges three numbers,
w1, w2, and b, in the direction that would reduce that penalty. After
a couple of hundred steps the loss has flattened and the line has
found its resting position, dividing the two classes with all
fourteen points on the correct side.

That is the whole trick, and it is worth stating plainly: the
decision boundary is not a shape you draw, it is the visible
consequence of three numbers. The equation shown under the readouts,
w1 x plus w2 y plus b equals zero, is the boundary. Everything above
the line is classified one way, everything below the other; the
shaded half-planes make the two sides visible. Learning a classifier
is learning those numbers, and the numbers arrive by a process that
in principle is just as mechanical as long division.

Now grab a point and drag it. The boundary chases. Move a circle a
little way toward the squares and the line tilts to keep it on the
correct side; move it deeper and the line tilts more. The model does
not care which point moved or why, it just wants the current loss to
go down, and it revises its three numbers accordingly. Drag a point
clean into the middle of the other cluster and something more
interesting happens: the boundary compromises. It shifts to reduce
total loss across the whole set, and one point ends up on the wrong
side of the line. The misclassified readout ticks to one. Loss levels
off higher than zero, because a straight line cannot honestly
separate that arrangement.

That failure is not a bug, it is the model being disciplined. A
classifier that twisted itself into a shape that got every point
right, including a single wanderer buried in the other class, would
be memorizing the wanderer instead of learning the rule. The next
topic, Confusion Matrix, is about counting exactly what a classifier
gets right and wrong; and Overfitting, which comes just before this
one, is about what happens when a model does contort itself to reach
every point, and why straightness, or something close to it, is often
the honest answer.

Honesty notes. Logistic regression can only draw straight boundaries,
which is enough here because the data is two-dimensional and roughly
separable. Real classifiers work in feature spaces with hundreds or
thousands of dimensions, where the "line" is a hyperplane you cannot
draw, and where curved boundaries come from stacking nonlinear
layers, the story told in Why Activations Matter. The math in this
figure is standard: sigmoid outputs, cross-entropy loss, batch
gradient descent at a fixed learning rate. Every gradient step is
computed live from the current positions of the points; nothing is
scripted.
